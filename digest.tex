\section{Introduction}

\textbf{§1: Industry Trend}
\begin{itemize}
    \item DC distribution emerging as key enabler for megawatt-scale racks in AI/HPC data centers
    \item Power densities pushing beyond 100 kW per rack toward MW-level compute infrastructure
    \item Battery storage integrated to buffer rapid power fluctuations — avoiding causing headache for grid operators
    \item Architectures at 400V and 800V with multi-source topologies for backup and fluctuations
\end{itemize}

\textbf{§2: Where Existing Standards Fall Short}
\begin{itemize}
    \item IEC 61660 (1997): calculation method for batteries, rectifiers, DC motors, capacitors
    \item Gap 1: DC/DC converters have no defined model — despite being central to modern DC systems
    \item Gap 2: No methodology to calculate voltage as function of time post-fault — critical for system uptime
    \item Standard designed for auxiliary systems, not megawatt-scale DC microgrids
\end{itemize}

\textbf{§3: The Gap — What Is Missing}
\begin{itemize}
    \item No methodology to perform short-circuit current calculations for complex DC systems with numerous converters, batteries, and charged capacitors
    \item Cannot prove by calculation that protection devices handle fault current levels
    \item Cannot verify protection acts fast enough to prevent voltage collapse
    \item Cannot verify selectivity of protection devices
    \item Essential for: sizing equipment, specifying protection devices, enabling inspection bodies to verify compliance
\end{itemize}

\textbf{§4: Technical Challenge}
\begin{itemize}
    \item DC fault behavior fundamentally differs from AC: exponential current rise, no natural zero crossing
    \item Each source type (battery, converter, capacitor) contributes with distinct transient characteristics
    \item Voltage collapse: limited energy in capacitors causes voltage to collapse within milliseconds — protection must act in sub-ms to single-digit ms range
    \item Availability: fast fault clearing essential for system uptime, preventing cascading failures
    \item Selectivity: protection scheme must isolate only the faulted section while maintaining power to unaffected loads
    \item Safety advantage: fast solid-state interruption significantly reduces arc flash hazards compared to AC systems
\end{itemize}

\textbf{§5: Need for Practical Accessibility}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Calculation method must be usable by installers, system designers, and inspection bodies
    \item Applicable in modern software packages without requiring expertise in differential equations
    \item Industry hesitates to adopt DC at scale due to safety concerns stemming from absence of established calculation methods and protection guidelines
\end{itemize}

\textbf{§6: Contribution}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Systematic simulation-based methodology for DC short-circuit current calculations
    \item Enables specifying and selecting protection devices based on calculated peak current, rise time and $I^2t$
    \item Validated approach for protection device sizing and withstand verification
    \item Applicable to complex multi-source DC topologies at megawatt scale
    \item Purpose: provide the essential stepping stone to prove protection adequacy by calculation
\end{itemize}
